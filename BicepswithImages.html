Biceps Tenotomy vs. Tenodesis: A Comprehensive Visual Guide

Figure 1: Anatomical overview of the shoulder joint highlighting the long head biceps tendon and surrounding structures
When facing biceps tendon pathology, patients and surgeons must choose between two primary surgical approaches: tenotomy and tenodesis. This comprehensive guide provides evidence-based insights with visual references to help understand these critical treatment decisions.

Understanding Biceps Tendon Anatomy

The long head of the biceps tendon (LHBT) originates from the superior labrum and travels through the bicipital groove of the humerus. When this tendon becomes damaged due to repetitive stress, age-related degeneration, or acute injury, surgical intervention may be necessary to restore function and eliminate pain.

Figure 2: Detailed cross-sectional anatomy showing biceps tendon origin at the superior labrum and its course through the shoulder joint

Understanding this anatomy is crucial for appreciating why both tenotomy and tenodesis can effectively address biceps pathology, albeit through different mechanisms and with varying outcomes.

Surgical Technique Comparison

Tenotomy Approach

Biceps tenotomy involves simply cutting the long head biceps tendon at its attachment to the superior labrum. This releases the damaged tendon, allowing it to retract down the arm where scar tissue forms naturally.

Figure 3: Arthroscopic view demonstrating the tenotomy procedure with tendon release at the superior labrum

Procedure Characteristics:

  • Minimally invasive arthroscopic approach
  • Procedure time: 10-20 minutes
  • Immediate pain relief through tendon release
  • Natural healing through scar tissue formation

Tenodesis Approach

Biceps tenodesis involves cutting the damaged portion of the tendon and then reattaching the healthy portion to a new location on the humerus, typically using screws, anchors, or other fixation devices.

Figure 4: Various tenodesis fixation methods showing interference screw technique (left) and suture anchor approach (right)

Procedure Characteristics:

  • Can be performed arthroscopically or through small open incision
  • Procedure time: 30-45 minutes
  • Preserves biceps length-tension relationship
  • Requires fixation hardware for tendon reattachment

Patient Outcomes and Evidence

Clinical Research Findings

Multiple high-quality studies have compared outcomes between tenotomy and tenodesis, providing valuable evidence for surgical decision-making.

Figure 5: Comparative outcomes data from recent meta-analysis showing patient satisfaction rates, "Popeye" deformity incidence, and functional scores

Key Research Findings:

  • Patient Satisfaction: Tenotomy 91% vs. Tenodesis 96% (Aflatooni et al., 2020)
  • "Popeye" Deformity: Tenotomy 43-47% vs. Tenodesis 9-33% (van Deurzen et al.)
  • Cramping Incidence: Tenotomy 20% vs. Tenodesis 8% (Aflatooni et al., 2020)
  • Return to Activity: Tenotomy 2-4 weeks vs. Tenodesis 3-6 months

Recovery Timeline and Expectations

Understanding the recovery process is essential for setting appropriate patient expectations and optimizing outcomes.

Figure 6: Recovery timeline comparison showing key milestones for both procedures from surgery through full recovery

The recovery timeline differences significantly impact patient selection. Tenotomy offers faster return to activities but with potential cosmetic changes, while tenodesis requires longer rehabilitation but better preserves biceps function and appearance.

Decision-Making Factors

The choice between tenotomy and tenodesis should be individualized based on multiple patient factors:

Figure 7: Evidence-based decision algorithm incorporating patient age, activity level, and individual preferences

Patient Selection Criteria:

  • Age: Younger patients (<60) often benefit from tenodesis
  • Activity Level: High-demand athletes typically prefer tenodesis
  • Cosmetic Concerns: Patients concerned about appearance favor tenodesis
  • Recovery Time: Those needing quick return may choose tenotomy
  • Medical Comorbidities: Complex medical conditions may favor simpler tenotomy

Conclusion

Both biceps tenotomy and tenodesis represent effective surgical options for addressing long head biceps tendon pathology. The choice between procedures should be made through shared decision-making, considering individual patient factors, activity demands, and personal preferences.

Recent research demonstrates high patient satisfaction rates with both approaches, though each carries distinct advantages and limitations. Understanding these differences, visualized through anatomical illustrations and outcome data, enables more informed treatment decisions and better patient outcomes.
